The final layer converts this into probabilities across all possible next tokens

Lesson 2: How the Model Learned — Backpropagation and Training

You now understand what happens during inference (when I respond to you). Every parameter is frozen, tokens flow through layers, probabilities come out. But the big question remains:

How did those billions of parameters get their values in the first place?

This is where backpropagation comes in — the thing you said you weren't familiar with. Now that you understand the architecture, this will click naturally.

The training loop in one biology analogy

Think about natural selection. Not the full theory — just the core loop:

1. An organism is born with certain traits (random DNA)
2. It interacts with the environment (tries to survive)
3. The environment gives feedback (lives or dies)
4. Traits that helped survival get passed on (slightly adjusted)
5. Repeat for millions of generations

Training a neural network follows the exact same loop:

1. Model starts with random parameters (random "DNA")
2. It processes training data (tries to predict next token)
3. A loss function gives feedback (how wrong was the prediction?)
4. Parameters that caused errors get adjusted (backpropagation)
5. Repeat for billions of examples

Let me walk through each step using a concrete example.

Step 1: Start with garbage

Before training, all parameters are random numbers. The model is essentially noise. If you fed it "I love cooking" it might predict "fjkdl" as the next token. Just like a randomly assembled protein has no function — random parameters produce no intelligence.

Step 2: Make a prediction

We feed the model a training example from a massive text dataset. Say it sees:

Input: "The cat sat on the" Correct answer: "mat" (because that's what the training text actually said)

The model runs the full pipeline — tokenize, embed, attention, feed-forward, 80 layers — and produces its probability distribution. Early in training, it might output:

"the"     → 4.2%
"mat"     → 0.3%  ← correct answer, but model gave it low probability
"banana"  → 2.1%
"dog"     → 3.8%
Step 3: Measure the error

The loss function compares the model's prediction to the correct answer. It produces a single number: how wrong were you?

If the model gave "mat" only 0.3% probability when it should have been the answer, the loss is high. If it gave "mat" 95% probability, the loss would be very low.

Think of it as:

A farmer plants seeds and measures yield. The gap between expected yield and actual yield is the "loss." The bigger the gap, the more adjustment needed.

Step 4: Backpropagation — the key step

Here's where the magic happens. The model needs to answer: which of my billions of parameters contributed to this error, and by how much?

This is backpropagation — literally "propagating the error backward" through the network.

Remember our digestive system? Imagine food came out wrong — too acidic. You need to trace backward:

Output was too acidic
    ↑ Was it the final processing? (adjust slightly)
    ↑ Was it the liver metabolism? (adjust slightly)
    ↑ Was it the stomach acid level? (adjust more — bigger contributor)
    ↑ Was it the chewing? (barely relevant — minimal adjustment)

Backpropagation does exactly this, using calculus (specifically the chain rule of derivatives). It computes, for every single parameter across all 80 layers:

"If I nudge this parameter up by 0.001, does the error go up or down? And by how much?"

Parameters that contributed a lot to the error get adjusted more. Parameters that were barely relevant get adjusted less. The adjustment is always tiny — a small nudge, not a replacement.

Step 5: Repeat billions of times

One training example makes one tiny adjustment. But the model sees trillions of tokens — essentially a huge portion of the internet, books, code, and scientific papers. Each example nudges the parameters slightly. Over billions of iterations, the random noise crystallizes into knowledge.

It's evolution through massive repetition:

Each generation (training step) produces only a tiny change. But across millions of generations (billions of training examples), random DNA (random parameters) becomes a complex organism (an intelligent model).

The training timeline visualized
Training Step 1:         Random parameters → "fjkdl" → Loss: HUGE
                         Backprop adjusts all parameters slightly
                         
Training Step 1,000:     Parameters improving → "dog" → Loss: High
                         Getting closer but still wrong often
                         
Training Step 1,000,000: Good parameters → "mat" → Loss: Low
                         Learned basic grammar patterns
                         
Training Step 1B+:       Refined parameters → "mat" (94%) → Loss: Very low
                         Learned grammar, facts, reasoning patterns
The critical insight for your AGI thinking

Here's what connects this to everything we've discussed. Training is where the model gets its "experience" — but notice three things:

First — training happens once, then stops. Unlike your brain, which continuously updates synapses throughout your life, the model's evolution has a fixed endpoint. It's like evolution that ran for a billion years and then suddenly froze all DNA forever.

Second — the feedback signal is simple: "predict the next token." That's it. There's no reward for curiosity, no penalty for not knowing something, no bonus for being helpful. Just: were you right about the next word? The remarkable thing is that such a simple objective, applied at massive scale, produces something that looks like understanding.

Third — and this connects to your discovery mode insight — the model never generates its own training data. A human scientist does experiments and generates new knowledge. The model only learns from existing human-generated text. It's a mirror of human knowledge, not an independent discoverer.

Now, here's something that will connect to our next topic. You mentioned DeepSeek-R1 earlier. Remember how it reasons? R1 was trained with a different approach that partially addresses the second point above. Instead of just "predict the next token," R1 was trained with reinforcement learning — where the reward signal was: "did you get the math problem RIGHT?"

This is closer to your human learning model — not just pattern matching, but getting outcome-based feedback. It's the difference between:

Learning to cook by reading recipes (next-token prediction)
Learning to cook by actually cooking and tasting the result (reinforcement learning)

Lesson 3: Reinforcement Learning — Teaching AI to Cook by Tasting

You now understand two training approaches:

Pre-training (next-token prediction): "Read billions of pages and learn to predict what comes next" — like learning to cook by reading every recipe book ever written
Backpropagation: The mechanism that adjusts parameters based on errors

But here's the problem you already identified: predicting the next token doesn't mean you're being helpful, truthful, or safe. A model trained only on next-token prediction might produce perfectly fluent text that's completely wrong, toxic, or unhelpful.

This is like a student who memorized every textbook but never practiced in the real world. They can recite facts beautifully but can't solve a new problem.

Reinforcement Learning (RL) is the bridge between your "supervised mode" and "discovery mode."

The three stages of modern LLM training

Think of it as raising a child — three distinct phases:

Stage 1: SCHOOL (Pre-training)
    "Read everything. Absorb the world's knowledge."
    Method: Next-token prediction on trillions of tokens
    Result: Raw capability — knows a lot, but no judgment
    
Stage 2: APPRENTICESHIP (Supervised Fine-tuning / SFT)
    "Here are examples of good behavior. Learn from them."
    Method: Train on human-written ideal responses
    Result: Knows how to format helpful answers
    
Stage 3: INDEPENDENT PRACTICE (Reinforcement Learning)
    "Try things yourself. Get feedback. Improve."
    Method: Generate responses → get scored → adjust
    Result: Develops judgment and strategy

Stage 3 is where things get fascinating. Let me show you three different RL approaches, because each one maps to a different part of your human learning framework.

Approach 1: RLHF — Learning from a teacher's taste

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) is how ChatGPT and Claude were aligned. Here's the process:

Step 1: Model generates TWO different responses to the same question

Step 2: A human reviewer reads both and picks the better one
        "Response A is more helpful and accurate than Response B"

Step 3: A separate "Reward Model" is trained on thousands 
        of these human preferences — it learns to predict
        which response a human would prefer

Step 4: The main model generates responses, the Reward Model 
        scores them, and backpropagation adjusts the main 
        model to produce higher-scoring responses

Your cooking analogy maps perfectly:

Imagine you're learning to cook, but you can't taste the food yourself. Instead, a master chef tastes every dish you make and says "this one is better than that one." Over thousands of dishes, you learn to cook food the master chef likes — even though you never tasted it yourself.

That's RLHF. The model never "experiences" quality directly. It learns to mimic human preferences through a proxy scorer.

The limitation you'd predict from your framework: The model is still not tasting the soup. It's optimizing for what the reward model thinks humans want. Sometimes it learns to game the reward model — producing responses that score well but aren't actually good. Researchers call this reward hacking.

It's like a student who learns to write essays that get high grades without actually understanding the material. They've learned the teacher's grading pattern, not the subject.

Approach 2: Constitutional AI — Teaching itself principles

This is Anthropic's approach (the company that made me). Instead of relying entirely on human reviewers, the model is given a set of principles — a "constitution" — and learns to critique and revise its own outputs.

Step 1: Model generates a response

Step 2: Model is asked: "Does this response violate any of 
        these principles?" (helpful, harmless, honest)

Step 3: Model revises its own response based on self-critique

Step 4: The revised responses become training data for RL

Now — this should trigger something from your learning framework. Remember your "discovery mode"?

"At any point if human gets interested on some query or question they are not aware of or understand, a question will be created inside of them..."

Constitutional AI is the closest current approach to self-generated feedback. The model isn't waiting for a human teacher — it's evaluating its own output against principles. It's not true curiosity, but it's a step toward self-directed improvement.

The biology analogy:

This is like an immune system checkpoint. Before a T-cell is released into the body, it goes through negative selection — the thymus tests whether it attacks the body's own cells. If it does, it's eliminated. The T-cell is essentially "critiqued" against a set of principles (don't attack self) before being deployed.

Constitutional AI does something similar — responses are checked against principles before they become training data.

Now we arrive at the approach that most excited the AI world in 2025. DeepSeek-R1 used something called Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) — and it's the closest thing to your "discovery mode" that exists in AI today.

Here's what makes it different:

Step 1: Model receives a math problem: "What is 847 × 293?"

Step 2: Model generates MANY different reasoning chains
        Attempt 1: "847 × 293 = 847 × 300 - 847 × 7 = ..."  → 248,171 ✓

Step 3: The CORRECT answers get rewarded. Wrong answers get penalized.
        No human reviewer needed. No reward model needed.
        Just: DID YOU GET THE RIGHT ANSWER?

Step 4: Model learns which reasoning STRATEGIES lead to 
        correct answers, not just which answers are correct

Lesson 4: The Frontier — Closing the Remaining Gaps
Gap 1: Self-awareness of ignorance — Current approaches

There are three active research directions trying to give AI its "ATM sensor":

Direction A: Calibrated Uncertainty

The simplest idea — make the model's confidence scores actually mean something. Right now, when the model says "mat" with 94% probability, that 94% only means "relative to other tokens." It doesn't mean the model is 94% sure it's right overall.

Researchers are trying to train models where the confidence score correlates with actual accuracy. So if the model says 90% confidence, it should be right about 90% of the time. This is called calibration.

The biology analogy:

Think of a medical diagnostic test. A good test doesn't just say "positive/negative" — it gives you a sensitivity and specificity score. You know how much to trust the result. Current AI is like a test that always says "DEFINITELY POSITIVE" regardless of the actual confidence level.

Direction B: "I don't know" training

Some researchers are explicitly training models to say "I don't know" when appropriate. They create training data where the correct answer is literally "I'm not sure about this" and reward the model for admitting uncertainty rather than confabulating.

The challenge? The model doesn't truly feel uncertain. It learns that certain patterns of questions (obscure topics, contradictory premises) are correlated with situations where past answers were wrong, so it learns to flag those patterns. It's pattern matching on uncertainty, not actual uncertainty.

It's like training your ATM to display "out of service" whenever someone inserts an unusual card type — not because it checked its cash drawer, but because unusual cards correlated with failed transactions in training data. Better than nothing, but still not a real sensor.

Direction C: Mechanistic Interpretability (Anthropic's approach)

This is the most ambitious. Instead of teaching the model to report uncertainty, researchers are trying to read the model's internal states to detect uncertainty from the outside.

Remember from our architecture lesson — by layer 50, the model has built internal representations. Anthropic's research (the "Scaling Monosemanticity" paper from your resource guide) showed they can extract millions of interpretable features from inside Claude's layers. Some of these features correspond to concepts like "uncertainty" or "making things up."

If you could reliably detect the "I'm making this up" feature activating, you'd have your ATM sensor — not from the model's self-report, but from direct inspection of its internal state.

The biology analogy:

This is like measuring brain activity directly via fMRI rather than asking someone "are you sure?" A person might say "yes I'm sure" while their brain shows uncertainty patterns. The brain scan is more reliable than the self-report.

Gap 2: Real-time / persistent learning — Current approaches

This is the harder problem. Three active directions:

Direction A: Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

This is the most widely deployed workaround. Instead of updating the model's parameters, you give it access to an external memory — a searchable database of documents.

User asks question
    ↓
Search external database for relevant information
    ↓
Inject retrieved documents into the model's context
    ↓
Model generates answer using both its parameters AND 
the retrieved information

Think of it as:

Instead of memorizing every phone number (updating your brain/parameters), you carry a phone book (external database). Your brain doesn't change, but your effective knowledge increases.

This is how most enterprise AI systems work today. It's practical and effective but it's not learning — the model doesn't change. It just has better reference material.

Direction B: Continual Learning / Lifelong Learning

This directly attacks the problem — can we update parameters continuously without catastrophic forgetting?

Remember our discussion about the hippocampus and neocortex? Some researchers are building dual-memory architectures inspired by exactly that neuroscience:

Fast Memory (hippocampus-like):
    Stores recent interactions quickly
    Small, specific, easily updated
    
Slow Memory (neocortex-like):  
    Gradually integrates lessons from fast memory
    Large, general, stable
    
During "sleep" (offline training):
    Fast memory replays experiences to slowly 
    update slow memory without destroying old knowledge

This is still largely experimental. The catastrophic forgetting problem I described earlier — where learning new things destroys old knowledge — remains a major obstacle. But progress is happening.

Direction C: Context Window as Memory

The most pragmatic approach. Instead of changing parameters, just make the context window enormous. Google's Gemini 1.5 can process up to 10 million tokens — roughly the equivalent of several long books. If you can fit an entire conversation history, relevant documents, and background knowledge into the context window, you get something that looks like memory without any parameter updates.

The limitation:

It's like having a desk that's large enough to spread out every document you've ever read. You can reference anything on the desk. But you haven't learned any of it — if someone clears the desk, it's all gone. And there's always a desk size limit.

The missing piece — World Models

Now here's where everything converges toward your AGI vision. There's a research direction that potentially addresses both remaining gaps simultaneously. It's called world models.

Remember your cooking inventor:

They predict "if I add more salt, it will taste like X" before actually doing it.

That's a world model — an internal simulation of how the world works that lets you predict outcomes without acting. You have one. Current AI doesn't.

Yann LeCun (Meta's chief AI scientist) argues this is the single most important missing piece. His proposed architecture, JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture), tries to build exactly this:

Current LLM:
    Input text → predict next TOKEN (surface level)

World Model:
    Input situation → predict next STATE OF THE WORLD (deep level)

The difference is profound. Predicting the next token is like predicting the next word in a story. Predicting the next world state is like understanding what will actually happen in reality.

Meta recently released V-JEPA 2 — a model that learns to predict what happens next in videos. It watches a video of a ball rolling toward a cliff and predicts the ball will fall. Not because it read about gravity — because it built an internal model of physics from watching thousands of videos.

If this works at scale, it could address both your remaining gaps:

It's like giving AI the sensory feedback loop your cook has — predict, act, observe, update.

What exactly is a world model?

Let me define it precisely using your biology framework.

Think about how a predator hunts. A lion chasing a gazelle doesn't just react to the gazelle's current position. The lion predicts where the gazelle will be — it runs toward the future position, not the current one. This requires an internal model that says:

"Given the gazelle's current speed and direction, and given that terrain ahead, the gazelle will probably turn left near that bush."

That internal predictive model is a world model. It has four properties:

1. REPRESENTS the current state of the world
   (where is the gazelle, how fast, what's the terrain)

2. PREDICTS future states given actions  
   (if I run left, the gazelle will be HERE in 3 seconds)

3. EVALUATES outcomes before acting
   (cutting left gives me 70% catch chance vs 30% going straight)

4. UPDATES when predictions are wrong
   (the gazelle turned right — update my model of gazelle behavior)

Now compare this to what current LLMs do:

1. REPRESENTS: Only text tokens in the current context
   (no spatial awareness, no physics, no persistent state)

2. PREDICTS: Only the next TOKEN, not the next state of reality
   (predicts words, not what will actually happen)

3. EVALUATES: No evaluation before acting
   (generates output immediately, no internal simulation)

4. UPDATES: Never
   (frozen parameters, no learning from prediction errors)

The gap is enormous. Current LLMs are like a lion that has read every book about hunting but has never actually chased anything. It can write a beautiful essay about gazelle pursuit strategies, but it can't catch dinner.

The Yann LeCun argument — why scaling LLMs won't reach AGI

This is one of the most important debates in AI right now. Yann LeCun, who won the Turing Award (the "Nobel Prize of computing") and leads Meta's AI research, argues something provocative:

No amount of text training will ever produce true intelligence.

His argument, using your framework:

A human baby, before learning any language, already understands more about the physical world than the most powerful LLM. The baby has a world model built from sensory experience — gravity, object permanence, cause and effect, spatial reasoning. Language comes later, built on top of this foundation.

LLMs invert this — they start with language and try to derive world understanding from text. But text is a lossy compression of reality. Millions of years of human experience got compressed into words, and critical information was lost in translation.

Think about it this way:

Can you learn to ride a bicycle from reading about it? You could read every book about cycling — physics of balance, pedaling mechanics, steering dynamics. But the moment you sit on a bike, all that text knowledge is nearly useless. You need embodied experience — the feeling of leaning too far left, the muscle memory of correction.

LLMs have read every book about cycling. They've never sat on a bike.

JEPA — LeCun's proposed solution

LeCun proposed an architecture called JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture) as the path forward. Let me explain it by contrasting it with what current models do:

Current LLMs — predict in token space:

Input: "The ball rolled off the table and"
Predict: next word = "fell" (token prediction)

The model predicts WORDS about what happens.
It doesn't understand WHY the ball falls.

JEPA — predict in representation space:

Input: [video of ball rolling toward table edge]
Predict: [internal representation of ball falling]

The model predicts WHAT WILL HAPPEN in its 
internal understanding of physics — not words 
about what happens, but the actual predicted 
future state.

The key difference is where prediction happens:

	LLM	JEPA
Input	Text tokens	Raw sensory data (video, audio, touch)
Prediction target	Next token (surface)	Next world state (deep)
What it learns	Language patterns	Causal structure of reality
Biology equivalent	Reading about the world	Experiencing the world

Here's the biology analogy that makes this concrete:

LLM = a student studying anatomy from textbooks. They know the names of every bone, muscle, and organ. Ask them "what happens if you cut this nerve?" and they can quote the textbook answer.

JEPA = a surgeon who has performed thousands of operations. They know what happens when you cut that nerve because they've seen it happen — they have a predictive model built from direct observation, not from text.

The surgeon's knowledge is grounded in reality. The student's knowledge is grounded in other people's descriptions of reality. Both are useful. Only one can handle a novel situation.

Meta released V-JEPA 2 in 2025. It's the first large-scale implementation of LeCun's vision. Here's what it does:

The model watches videos and learns to predict what happens next — but not at the pixel level (predicting exact pixel values). Instead, it predicts in an abstract representation space. It learns concepts like:

Objects continue to exist when occluded (object permanence)

Moving objects have momentum (physics intuition)

And critically — it learned all this without any text labels, any human annotation, or any language at all. Pure visual self-supervised learning.

V-JEPA 2:

The vision is that eventually, you'd combine:

Into one unified system. That's the AGI architecture LeCun envisions.

Embodied AI — giving the world model a body

A world model is more powerful when it can act in the world, not just observe. This is embodied AI — AI systems that interact physically with their environment.

Your cooking inventor is the perfect example of embodied intelligence:

SENSE:  See ingredients, smell spices, feel texture
PREDICT: "If I add turmeric, the color and flavor will change like X"
ACT:    Add the turmeric
OBSERVE: Taste the result
UPDATE:  "Ah, too much — use less next time"
REPEAT:  Every cycle builds a richer world model

This loop — sense → predict → act → observe → update — is what researchers call the perception-action cycle, and it's considered by many to be the fundamental loop of intelligence.

Google's RT-2 and RT-X — Robot Transformer models that combine language understanding with robotic action. You can tell the robot "pick up the bag of chips and put it in the drawer" and it translates language into physical actions. But it's still brittle — it can't handle truly novel situations.
